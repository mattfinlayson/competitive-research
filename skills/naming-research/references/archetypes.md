# Naming Archetypes Reference

Deep dive into naming archetype patterns for competitive analysis.

## The Five Archetypes

### 1. Literal/Descriptive

**Pattern**: Name directly describes what the product does or who it's for.

**Examples**:
- SubProcess, ProcessLog, DataTrack
- OneTrust (trust + inclusive), ComplyDog (compliance + friendly)

**Characteristics**:
- Clear value proposition
- SEO-friendly (keywords)
- Easy to understand

**Pros**:
- Immediately clear what the product does
- Good for technical/professional audiences
- Strong keyword SEO

**Cons**:
- Can feel boring or corporate
- Harder to trademark
- Easy to confuse with DIY solutions
- Limited emotional range

**When to use**:
- Enterprise/B2B products where clarity > memorability
- Technical tools where specificity matters
- Compliance-heavy domains where precision is valued

### 2. Evocative/Action-Oriented

**Pattern**: Name evokes an action, emotion, or concept.

**Examples**:
- Attest, Certify, Vouch, Witness
- Osano (evokes "oh say", consent)
- Vanta (evokes "vantage", advantage)

**Characteristics**:
- Strong verbs or adjectives
- Trust connotations common
- Memorable and distinctive

**Pros**:
- Memorable and distinctive
- Emotional resonance
- Good for trust/compliance domains
- Easier to trademark than descriptive

**Cons**:
- May limit perceived scope (e.g., "attest" = proof only)
- Requires brand-building to convey meaning
- Can feel generic if overused

**When to use**:
- Products where trust is central
- Compliance/verification domains
- When you want to own a concept

### 3. Abstract/Made-up

**Pattern**: invented words or repurposed terms with no direct meaning.

**Examples**:
- Cohesion, Trustflow, Verifi, Provenly
- Salesforce (abstract), Zendesk (abstract)

**Characteristics**:
- Unique and ownable
- Requires brand investment
- Can convey multiple meanings

**Pros**:
- Unique and trademarkable
- Full brand control
- Can scale to multiple domains

**Cons**:
- Requires significant brand-building
- Initial meaning unclear
- May feel cold or impersonal

**When to use**:
- Products with broad scope
- Platforms that may expand
- When you have resources for brand-building

### 4. Compound/Hybrid

**Pattern**: Combines two or more meaningful words.

**Examples**:
- TrustStack, ConsentBase, SubProcure
- DataDog (data + dog mascot)
- GitHub (git + hub)

**Characteristics**:
- Combines clarity with uniqueness
- Often modern/technical feel
- Can be trademarked

**Pros**:
- Combines clarity with uniqueness
- Easier to trademark than descriptive
- Modern, technical feel

**Cons**:
- Can feel corporate or dated
- May be harder to pronounce
- Limited flexibility

**When to use**:
- Technical tools and platforms
- When combining concepts matters
- Modern/technical positioning

### 5. Short/Punchy

**Pattern**: Short, memorable names, often single words.

**Examples**:
- Prove, Vouch, Trusty, Attest
- Slack, Zoom, Notion, Figma

**Characteristics**:
- Memorable and distinctive
- Often dictionary words
- Modern and clean

**Pros**:
- Highly memorable
- Easy to say and spell
- Modern, clean feel

**Cons**:
- Often taken as dictionary words
- Domain availability challenges
- May lack depth

**When to use**:
- Consumer-facing products
- Modern SaaS
- When memorability is key

## Competitive Analysis Framework

When analyzing competitors, categorize them by archetype:

1. **Survey the landscape**: List all competitors in the domain
2. **Categorize each**: Assign to one of the 5 archetypes
3. **Note patterns**: Which archetypes dominate?
4. **Identify gaps**: Which archetypes are underserved?
5. **Assess resonance**: Which names do users remember?

## Key Questions

- What archetypes do competitors use?
- Which archetypes resonate most with users?
- Are there underserved archetypes in the space?
- Does your name choice differentiate or blend in?

## Red Flags

- Copying a dominant archetype too closely
- Using an archetype that conflicts with brand positioning
- Choosing a descriptive name in a crowded space
- Selecting an abstract name without resources for brand-building
